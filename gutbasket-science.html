Our Story · 02

The bacteria do the work. We just give them the right room.

Lacto-fermentation isn't a craft secret. It's a chemistry sequence — salt creates a selective environment, lactic acid bacteria win, sugars become acid, the food is preserved alive. Here's exactly what happens inside the jar, and why it matters for your gut.

Lacto-fermentation in 60 seconds

What's actually happening in the jar.

Four steps. Four days. The same chemistry that has preserved cabbage in Korea, mustard greens in China, milk in the Caucasus, and black carrots in Punjab for centuries.

Salt creates a gate

2–3% salt by weight makes the brine inhospitable to most bacteria, moulds, and yeasts. It does not bother lactic acid bacteria (LAB). They can swim. The competition can't.

LAB eat the sugars

Vegetables, fruits, and grains carry wild LAB on their skin. The dominant species — Lactobacillus plantarum, Leuconostoc mesenteroides, Pediococcus — start consuming naturally-present sugars.

pH drops below 4.0

LAB excrete lactic acid as a by-product. The brine acidifies. By Day 4, pH is around 3.5–3.8 — too acidic for spoilage organisms, just right for LAB.

Preserved, alive, bioactive

The food is now shelf-stable in the fridge, full of live cultures, plus the metabolites LAB produced along the way (vitamin K2, B12 traces, postbiotics, bioactive peptides).

The three biotics

Probiotic, prebiotic, postbiotic — explained.

The category mixes these up constantly. Here's how they actually relate.

🦠

Probiotic

The live bacteria themselves. Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, Pediococcus, Leuconostoc, Streptococcus. Found in any genuinely fermented food eaten cold or room-temp. Heating kills them.

🌾

Prebiotic

The food for those bacteria — fibres your body can't digest, but LAB can. Inulin (chicory root), FOS (fruit oligosaccharides), resistant starch (cooled rice, green banana). Our Ferment Boost is prebiotic.

⚗️

Postbiotic

The by-products bacteria leave behind — short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, acetate, propionate), bioactive peptides, exopolysaccharides. These are increasingly understood to do as much gut work as the bacteria themselves.

Why salt brine

Vinegar pickles aren't fermented.

Vinegar (acetic acid) is what's left over after fermentation has already finished — and it's anti-microbial enough to kill the LAB you want.

A vinegar pickle is preserved. A salt-brine ferment is alive. They taste roughly similar, which is how the lie about "fermented pickles" runs unchecked on most Indian shelves.

Every GutBasket kit ships with non-iodised salt and no vinegar. Period.

Strain spotlight

The four organisms doing most of the work.

Wild fermentation isn't random. Across most vegetable, drink, and grain ferments, four genera dominate the succession. We've mapped what each is doing in our kits.

L. plantarum

The closer.

The most studied LAB on Earth. Tolerates pH 3.5, dominates the late-fermentation phase. Linked in trials to reduced bloating, IBS-symptom relief, and improved gut-barrier integrity. Present in achaar, kanji, kimchi, sauerkraut.

L. brevis

The flavour-builder.

Produces complex aroma compounds and small amounts of CO₂. Responsible for the slight fizz in kanji and the depth in mature kimchi. Also produces GABA, a neurotransmitter linked to mood-axis effects.

Leuconostoc mesenteroides

The starter.

Dominates Days 1–3 of most veggie ferments. Heterofermentative — produces lactic acid plus ethanol plus CO₂. Once pH drops below 4, it bows out and L. plantarum takes over.

Pediococcus pentosaceus

The middle act.

Active in days 3–7. Highly tolerant of salt and acid. Important in fermented Indian batters (idli, dosa) and in mature kanji. Linked to immunomodulatory effects in animal models.
