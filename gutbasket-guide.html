SECTION 1 OF 6

Welcome to fermentation

20 min
▶
Why fermentation, and why now

Before we touch a jar, you need to know what you're doing and why. This first section is mindset and basics — no shopping yet.

What you'll learn: What lacto-fermentation actually is. Why salt is the only "preservative" you need. What "live cultures" really means in your gut.

Fermentation has been around for as long as humans have been preserving food. It predates refrigeration, predates canning, predates everything. And yet most modern Indian kitchens have forgotten it.

In the next 30 days you're going to bring it back. By day 30 you'll have three live ferments going. You'll know how to read them, how to fix them, and how to keep them going.

KEY TAKEAWAYS
  • Lacto-fermentation = salt + vegetables + time. No special equipment needed beyond a jar.
  • Salt at 2% by weight is the "referee" — keeps bad bacteria out, lets good ones thrive.
  • Live cultures = beneficial bacteria. They survive your stomach acid and reach your gut.

SECTION 2 OF 6

Setting up your kitchen lab

30 min
▶
Your fermentation toolkit

You don't need a "lab". You need 4 things: jar, weight, airlock, salt. This section walks through what to get, what to skip, and how much to spend.

The essential 4: Wide-mouth glass jar (1L or 2L). Glass weight that fits inside. Airlock with a silicone grommet. Sea salt or rock salt — never iodised.

Things you don't need: Fancy fermentation crocks (₹4,000+). Specialty starter cultures (the cabbage already has them). Distilled water (filtered tap is fine).

Total cost if you buy everything separately: about ₹1,200. If you buy our kit: ₹1,499 with the spice mixes already measured. Up to you — both work.

KEY TAKEAWAYS
  • Wide-mouth jars only. Narrow-mouth jars trap vegetables above the brine.
  • Use sea salt or rock salt. Iodised salt kills the bacteria you're trying to grow.
  • Filtered water — chlorinated tap water can stall fermentation.
→

SECTION 3 OF 6

Your first batch: sauerkraut

45 min hands-on, 7 days waiting
▶
Sauerkraut — the fermenter's first kiss

The most forgiving ferment in the world. One ingredient (cabbage), one preservative (salt), one container (jar). You will not fail at this. And on day 7, you'll eat real probiotic food you made yourself.

Day 0: Shred 1kg cabbage finely. Add 20g salt. Massage by hand for 5 minutes — the salt pulls water out of the cabbage, this is your brine. Pack tightly into the jar, push down so the brine covers all the cabbage. Glass weight on top. Airlock on. Done.

Day 1: Check that vegetables are still under brine. Smell — should be cabbage-y.

Day 3: Bubbles in the brine. Slightly cloudy. Smell shifting — sharper, more sour.

Day 7: Open. Taste a small piece. If you like the tang, refrigerate. If you want more tang, leave another 2-3 days.

KEY TAKEAWAYS
  • Salt = 2% of the cabbage weight. 1kg cabbage = 20g salt.
  • The brine MUST cover all cabbage. Anything above the brine line gets mould.
  • Bubbles by day 3 = working. No bubbles by day 4 = check temperature.
→
YOUR NEXT ACTION
Make 1kg of sauerkraut. Set a daily reminder to check the jar. Don't open it until day 7.
SECTION 4 OF 6

Reading your ferment

15 min
▶
Signs of a healthy ferment

By now you're a few days in. Things are happening in the jar. This section teaches you what to look for, what's normal, what's alarming, and what's actually fine.

Cloudy brine: Normal. The bacteria are multiplying. Brine clears slightly after fermentation completes.

Bubbles: Good. Especially days 3-5. Means the bacteria are producing CO2. Should slow down by day 6-7.

White film on the surface: Probably kahm yeast. Harmless. Skim and continue.

Fuzzy patches in any color: Probably mould. Most likely cause: vegetables above the brine line. Throw out and start again.

Sour, sharp smell: Excellent. That's lactic acid.

Putrid smell, rotten egg, ammonia: Throw out. Something went wrong.

KEY TAKEAWAYS
  • Cloudy ≠ bad. Cloudy = bacteria active.
  • White film = kahm yeast = scrape and continue.
  • Fuzzy = mould = throw out (most of the time).
  • Putrid smell = throw out always.
→
YOUR NEXT ACTION
Open your sauerkraut jar briefly on day 5. Look. Smell. Match what you see to this section. Record observations in a kitchen notebook.
SECTION 5 OF 6

Branching out: kanji and achaar

60 min hands-on, 5-7 days waiting
▶
Indian ferments — kanji and achaar

You've done sauerkraut. Now we make ferments that taste like home. Kanji — the Punjabi probiotic drink. Achaar — but the lacto-fermented kind, not the oil-preserved kind.

Kanji (5 days): 500g black or red carrots, sliced. 1L filtered water. 1 tbsp cracked mustard. 1 tsp kala namak. 1 tsp regular salt. Pack everything in the jar. Airlock. Wait 5 days. Strain. Drink.

Raw mango achaar (5-7 days): 900g raw mangoes cubed. 20g salt. 8g black mustard. 7g red chilli. 6g fennel. 3g turmeric. Mix, pack, weight, airlock. Wait 5-7 days.

Both follow the same fundamental pattern: vegetables + salt + spices + water + time. Once you get the pattern, you can ferment almost anything.

KEY TAKEAWAYS
  • Different vegetables ferment at different rates. Cabbage = 7 days. Mango = 5 days. Carrots = 5 days.
  • The pattern is universal: 2% salt, submerged vegetables, airlock, time.
  • You can substitute almost anything. Cucumbers. Cauliflower. Beetroot. Daikon.
→
YOUR NEXT ACTION
Make kanji and achaar. Run all three ferments simultaneously (sauerkraut, kanji, achaar). Label the jars with the start date. By the end of this section you'll have 3 active ferments.
SECTION 6 OF 6

Going forward

20 min
▶
Building a fermentation practice

Day 30. You have three live ferments. You've eaten food you made with bacteria. Now what?

Eat them daily: 1-2 tablespoons of fermented food per meal. Sauerkraut on a sandwich. Achaar with dal-rice. Kanji as a digestive shot before lunch.

Keep one batch always going: When one finishes, start another. The kitchen always has at least one active ferment.

Experiment: Try fermenting whatever's in season. Daikon in winter. Mangoes in summer. Carrots year-round.

Read more: Sandor Ellix Katz, "The Art of Fermentation". Best book on the subject.

Track what works: Notebook. Note what worked, what failed, why. Your batches improve every month.

KEY TAKEAWAYS
  • Daily consumption matters more than perfect technique.
  • A "fermentation practice" beats a "fermentation project".
  • You've done the hardest part — starting.
→
